Journeys into Japanese Culture, the 'Underwater Pyramid' and Oahspe

A traveller's account of sacred sites, indigenous rituals, ancient linguistics and one remarkable meeting with Professor Masaaki Kimura — the marine geologist who dedicated his career to studying the submerged structure at Yonaguni.

Introductory Background

The enigmatic structure, described by some as 'pyramid-like', at Iseki Point, Yonaguni island, Japan — the so-called Yonaguni Monument — was discovered in 1985 by dive tour operator Kihachiro Aratake. The Morien Institute takes the view that, following the dramatic series of rises in sea levels that marked the ending of the last Ice Age, it is inevitable that more evidence of ancient civilisation will be discovered on the continental shelves and shallow seas around the world.

The Yonaguni Monument is just one of a number of underwater megalithic structures in a 'complex' stretching for many hundreds of miles northeast of Taiwan. As sea levels rose at the end of the last Ice Age, the dry land once between the Chinese mainland and Japan was inundated.

The Debate — Manmade or Natural?

At a conference in Japan, Japanese geologists and archaeologists argued the sunken pyramid structure is man-made. It appears to be a construction of wide terraces, ramps and large steps. American geologists, however, contend it is a natural formation. Japanese scientists have documented tool marks on the stones and found tools nearby; a small carved stairway appears to render the natural-formation theory implausible.

The debate was further nuanced by Dr. Robert M. Schoch, a geologist who has dived the structure multiple times since 1997:

We should also consider the possibility that the Yonaguni Monument is fundamentally a natural structure that was utilized, enhanced, and modified by humans in ancient times. — Dr. Robert M. Schoch, 1999

This kind of activity — known as 'terra-forming' — appears to have been widely practised in the ancient world: nature suggests a shape, and human hands modify it for ritual or practical purposes.

The problem for western scientists is that it implies an unknown eastern culture had developed a high degree of organisation thousands of years before the earliest western civilisations. Geologically, the Yonaguni pyramid sank at the end of the last ice age, around ten thousand years ago.

Studies conducted over more than a decade by Professor Masaaki Kimura, marine geologist at the Department of Physics and Earth Sciences at the University of the Ryukyus, Okinawa, have been central to the debate. In September 1997, Dr. Schoch dived on the structure for the first time, invited there by Graham Hancock, who was then researching Heaven's Mirror for a television series.

"In Okinawan folklore, there are tales of traditional gods and a land of the gods called Nirai-Kanai, an unknown faraway land from where happiness is brought. Kimura said the Yonaguni Monument may have been built to serve a similar deity."

— The Japan Times, July 19, 2000

Structures have since been discovered underwater off other Japanese islands such as Chatan and Kerama, and walls — possibly ancient roads — have been found in the Straits of Taiwan, 20–30 feet underwater between Taiwan and mainland China.

Crazed Spirit

I went twice to Ainu rituals. The first was at a small, slightly touristy but obscure Ainu settlement. They performed it in their grass-roofed lodge with no walls. The women were at one end, the men at the other. A young male priest led the men in the actual ritual, but an overweight old shaman woman took charge of the supernatural parts.

A few feet outside were poles with wooden shavings streaming down their sides. These sticks are called inau (singular) and nusa (plural), treated with great reverence. The ritual went for maybe an hour. It ended with libations of rice beer served in lacquerware libation bowls — they passed them around, ritually sprinkling sake in three directions.

The old lady made out images in the smoke of the fire and said a lot in Japanese. A photo handed around appeared to show a light in the smoke resembling an angel or a figure of Christ. They shared the food offerings with us — some of which I could eat, since my friends confirmed it was vegan.

The second ritual took place in an ancient Ainu cemetery. We began by clearing weeds and laying out plastic. As the ritual proceeded, the same large shaman woman began calling, I believe, on the world of spirit. One of the group's mediums gave the appearance of being entranced. Then one of ours — a young, eccentric, spontaneous musical man — began to be possessed.

Bear Story

After the Rainbow gathering I went to Daisetsuzan National Park. I got off the bus at Tenninkyo Onsen and found my way to the trail head. I crossed over a barrier that put my intended walk off limits. Just before dark I reached the giant cliffs at the top of the gorge. I had seen tracks which I took to be deer, dismissing the thought that it might be a bear. At the gathering, Koji had spoken of Ainu who wouldn't walk in the forest because of bears — he said that in just that year, six people had already been killed.

I stopped dead still, and so did it. It could only be a bear.

The snow had covered up the track. After a frantic effort to find it again, I spotted electricity pylons in the distance and gambled they could only lead somewhere. Tramping through the snow, I was nearing them when I heard sudden feet stamping — with such thuds that the ground seemed to shake.

It was a long minute or two before I took the next step. I glimpsed the bear and was relieved that it didn't follow. I got to the pylons and found a cleared path, so kept going. But there was no way to leave — either side was snow and bushes. Then I heard a sound and before I knew it, came face to face with the bear coming towards me. We were maybe twenty feet apart, maybe double that.

🐻 Encounter

I somehow didn't see his full body, but clearly remember staring eye to eye — seeing his eyes become confused, rolling around. He turned his head, his body followed, and he ran back down the path. It was too quick for fear to set in. I paused a while, then moved on in the same direction as the bear, since I had no alternative. Eventually I saw his tracks move into the bush.

It wasn't until the next day that I became relaxed enough to contemplate the great fear of what I had actually gone through.

The power lines led to Asahidake Onsen, where I washed in a lukewarm stream. The young ranger was serious but friendly, and when I told him the story, he said that being so still had saved my life — had I run, the bear would have attacked.

A Little about Hokkaido

Much of Hokkaido is not wild and rugged as I had expected, but hilly — newly settled Japanese country communities with bitumen roads and plain modern houses, very monotonous and not worth seeing. But the national parks are quite spectacular with lofty Alpine peaks, gorges and waterfalls, blazing with colour in autumn.

I went to the touristy Ainu settlement in the outskirts of Akan city. There is a daily performance worth seeing — it demonstrates a need to practise rites and ceremonies. They are elegant and unifying for the Ainu culture, a statement of identity. They place an emphasis on owl images, which is puzzling until you learn that Chikap Kamui is the Ainu kamui (god) of owls and the land.

Kamui — Ainu Cosmology

Kamui are similar to Japanese kami but often more specifically defined. Kandakoro Kamui is the kamui of the sky — the prime originator of Ainu mythology, responsible either directly or indirectly for the creation of all things. He is not a supreme being but a background figure: necessary for the world's creation, yet playing only a small part in subsequent events, often as a mediator. He appointed Moshirikara Kamui to shape the earth, preparing it for habitation by humankind.

The Ainu had no writing system, and much of their mythology was passed down orally in the form of kamui yukar (deity epics) — long verses traditionally recounted by singers at a gathering.

While at Kushiro Shitsugen National Park, I came across reconstructed pit houses, a little off the beaten track. The oldest piece of wood used in Jomon construction — from the Yokoo site in Oita prefecture — is dated to 10,000 years ago. The 3.8-metre piece of wood, thought to be a roof beam from a house built on stilts, had six circular joint holes.

I have since found out about stone circles in Japan. The most well-known, along with the Oyu circle, is Oshiyoro in Otaru, Hokkaido — an oval shape 33m × 22m with a small circle inside.

Stone Circles in Japan

Ceremonial Stone Circles — Jōmon Period

Ceremonial stone circles first appeared at the beginning of the Jomon era (ca. 10,000 BC – 300 BC). Hundreds have been found all over Japan. Some appear to have been monuments aligned to celestial bodies — experts suggest an annual calendar based on seasons and the waxing and waning of the moon, likely used to coordinate trade gatherings.

The Kanayama megaliths comprise three main sites: the rising sun at the summer solstice illuminates the first, while the winter solstice illuminates the third — similar to Stonehenge's Heel Stone alignment.

The 4,000-year-old Oyu stone circle comprises two large circles: the Nonakado (42 m diameter) and the Manza (48 m). Rocks were carried from the Akuya River, about five kilometres east of the site. Artefacts including bowls, urns, charms and tools have also been excavated.

Stone circles in Japan are thought to be unique to the region and to have been developed independently of other parts of East Asia — the features found elsewhere differ markedly in character.

Northern, Central & Western Honshu in a Week

I activated my 7-day Japan rail pass and in one day went from Kushiro to Sendai, taking a brief look at the Sapporo Ainu museum and seeing the southern volcanoes of Hokkaido from the train. The next day I was on Haguro-san, one of the holy mountains of Dewa Sanzan — worshipped for centuries by yamabushi, followers of Shugendo.

I followed a man in yamabushi attire leading a group into the Saikan lodge where I watched a video about the ascetic, mountain-dwelling sect that "seeks oneness with kami". Shugendo, meaning "the path of training and testing", was founded 1,300 years ago. It is considered the foundation of Vajrayana in Japan, and its teaching holds that "the mountain is considered as the supernatural mandala."

Afterwards I walked down the stone steps lined with ancient cedars and came across the 14th-century five-storey pagoda shown here.

Travelling on quickly, I reached Nagano late at night. I awoke at dawn and walked to the Zenkoji temple to find the morning service about to begin. The early-morning blessing ceremony was given by a high priest and a high priestess. Zenko-ji was founded before Buddhism was split into different sects, and currently belongs to both the Tendai and Jodo Shu schools.

Tendai & Jodo Shu Buddhism

Tendai is a descendant of the Chinese Tiantai school. It is rooted in Mahayana Buddhism — the view that Buddha-hood is intrinsic in all things, and that the phenomenal world is an expression of Dharma. Tendai doctrine crucially allowed reconciliation with Shinto, meaning that kami are seen as manifestations of universal Buddha-hood that descend to teach mankind.

Jodo Shu (Pure Land School) was established in 1175 by the ex-Tendai monk Honen. It is the most widely practised branch of Buddhism in Japan. Its central teaching involves devotion to Amida Buddha as expressed through the nembutsu, by which a being may be reborn in the Pure Land through Amida's compassion.

A couple of months later, at the end of my journey, I stayed in the mountains of Kyoto and came down to meditate in the main temple at Chion-in, the highest temple of Jodo Shu. There was a liberty that allowed the spirit essence of the sanctuary to pass through me, and a feeling of oneness towards all members of the social order.

After Nagano I saw my first castle at Matsumoto, then visited Tokyo for the afternoon before sleeping on the river bank near Nikko. On day 4, I toured the Nikko temple complex. On day 5, I slept in the forest at Kamakura, and a morning walker made himself my guide to some of the shrines.

The ordinary town of Ise becomes intensely beautiful upon reaching the Ise Jingu shrine complexes of Naiku and Geku — situated within the Ise-Shima National Park.

Ise Grand Shrine

Ise Jingu is a Shinto shrine dedicated to goddess Amaterasu-omikami. The Inner Shrine (Naiku) is arguably Shinto's holiest site — the High Priest or Priestess must come from the Japanese Imperial Family. It is said to hold the Sacred Mirror, one of three sacred objects given to the first emperor by the gods.

Following a tradition begun in 690 AD, the shrine buildings are dismantled every 20 years and exact replicas built on an adjacent site — so that the buildings are forever new and forever ancient. It is an expression of the Shinto belief in the death and renewal of nature, the impermanence of all things (wabi-sabi), and a means of passing building techniques from generation to generation.

I arrived in Kagoshima at about midnight — the end of my rail pass. It was really amazing to have fit all these places from Kushiro, Hokkaido to Kagoshima, Kyushu into one week. I was worn out and went to sleep in a paddock next to the station. Finally I could slow down.

I bought a ferry ticket that gives seven days to stop off at any island on the route, stopping at Tokunoshima, Okinoerabu-jima, Yoronto and getting off at northern Okinawa. But the islands south of Okinawa are where the real tropical feeling lies, with thick rainforest and coral reef in the surrounding azure waters.

Early Names of Japan

The fleet of two ships carried to the north was named Yista, which in the Whaga tongue was Zha'Pan, which is the same country that is to this day called Japan, signifying Relict of the Continent of Pan, for it lay to the north, where the land was cleaved in twain. — The Lords' First Book Ch. I of Oahspe

The ancient name of Whaga could possibly still be existent in the names Wōguó/Wakoku (lit. "Japan country") — an old Chinese/Japanese name of Japan.

The Japanese name for Japan is written using two kanji characters: 日 (nichi) meaning "day" or "sun", and 本 (hon) meaning "origin", "base" or "root". The compound meaning can become "original day" to signify where the first day of man began — but the accepted meaning is "sun-origin" or "Land of the Rising Sun."

The Etymology of Japan's Many Names

The English word "Japan" came to the West from early trade routes. The early Mandarin Chinese or Wu Chinese word for Japan was recorded by Marco Polo as Cipangu. The modern Shanghainese pronunciation of 日本 is still Zeppen — compare this with Oahspe's Zha'Pan. The old Malay word Jepang was borrowed from a Chinese language and encountered by Portuguese traders in Malacca in the 16th century.

Nippon appeared in history only at the end of the 7th century. The Old Book of Tang states that the Japanese envoy disliked his country's name Woguo 倭國 — compare Woguo with Whaga — and changed it to Nippon, or "Origin of the Sun."

Wa — The Oldest Recorded Name of Japan

Japanese 倭 (Wa, "Japan, Japanese", from Chinese Wō) is the oldest recorded name of Japan. Chinese, Korean, and Japanese scribes regularly wrote Wa or Yamato with this character until the 8th century, when the Japanese found fault with it and replaced it with 和, meaning "harmony, peace, balance."

The Meaning of "Wa" — Two Etymologies

The character 倭 combines 人/亻 (human, person) and a phonetic element meaning "bent." Interpretations split into two etymologies:

  • "Submissive / obedient" — the Shuowen Jiezi dictionary (121 AD) defines it as "obedient/submissive appearance." Bowing is noted in all early historical references to Japan.
  • "Dwarf / short person" — this reading led to the Chinese character being viewed as derogatory, prompting the switch to 和.

Koji Nakayama offers a third reading: wēi = "winding / very far away" — and translates Wo as "separated from the continent."

The Japanese first-person pronoun for "my/our" is waga 我. Compare this with Oahspe's Whaga.

The Japanese 'Ryu' character means dragon. Therefore Ryu-kyu means nine dragons.
Okina = big  |  Wa = ring  →  Okina-Wa means 'big ring.'

Historical Evidence for the Existence of Wa

Chinese Chronicles — From the Shan Hai Jing to the Tang Shu

Possibly the earliest record of Wō occurs in the Shan Hai Jing 山海經, dating from possibly 300 BC. The Han Shu (Book of Han, ca. 82 AD) records: "Beyond Lo-lang in the sea, there are the people of Wo. They comprise more than one hundred communities."

The Wei Zhi (ca. 297 AD) provides detailed first-hand accounts by Chinese envoys: "The people of Wa dwell in the middle of the ocean on the mountainous islands southeast of Tai-fang. They formerly comprised more than one hundred communities."

According to the Book of Later Han, in 57 AD Emperor Guangwu granted Nakoku an imperial seal made of gold. This seal was discovered in 1784 by an Edo period farmer on the island of Shikanoshima (Hakata Bay), helping to verify the existence of Nakoku which was otherwise known only from ancient chronicles.

The Wei Zhi — On Tattooing and Dragons

One Wei Zhi passage relates Wa tattooing with legendary King Shao Kang of the Xia Dynasty:

Men great and small, all tattoo their faces and decorate their bodies with designs. A son of the ruler, Shao-k'ang of Hsia, cut his hair and decorated his body with designs in order to avoid the attack of serpents and dragons. The Wa, who are fond of diving into the water to get fish and shells, also decorated their bodies in order to keep away large fish and waterfowl. Later, however, the designs became merely ornamental.

Yamadai, Yamato and Nara — the Yayoi Period

The Yayoi Period (500 BC – 300 AD)

The Yayoi period followed the Jomon period (14,000 BC – 500 BC). Direct comparisons between Jomon and Yayoi skeletons show clear physical differences: the Jomon tended to be shorter with wide-set eyes and pronounced facial topography; Yayoi people averaged an inch or two taller with close-set eyes, high narrow faces and flat browridges. By the Kofun period, almost all excavated Japanese skeletons (except Ainu and prehistoric Okinawans) resemble modern Japanese. The Ainu are believed to be relatively purer descendants of the Jomon people.

Archaeologists found many similarities between Yayoi skulls and remains from early Han Dynasty coastal Jiangsu province. Third-century Chinese sources reported that the Wa people lived on raw fish, vegetables and rice; clapped their hands in worship (still done in Shinto shrines today); and built earthen grave mounds.

According to the Wei Zhi, Himiko assumed the throne of Wa as a spiritual leader after a large civil war. The Sanguo Zhi records Yamataikoku 邪馬台国 as the domain of shaman Queen Himiko. While historians disagree over the location of Yamatai, linguists concur that the name was an early form of Japanese Yamato 大和.

In 1989, archaeologists discovered a giant Yayoi-era complex at the Yoshinogari site in Saga Prefecture. They excavated 33 bronze mirrors — the Wei Zhi records presenting "one hundred bronze mirrors" to Queen Himiko in 238 AD.

Dragons and Mirrors

30 June 2009 — Before leaving Okinawa for Ishigaki Island

In the book The Essence of Shinto by Motohisa Yamakage, it is explained that before the Meiji restoration in 1868 there was never a national or state Shinto. Ko-Shinto, the early form prior to Buddhist and Taoist influence, was never organised — each village had an organisation for its festivals, each powerful shrine had an organisation for its rituals, but they acted as independent entities.

In the past I found no justifiable meaning for Kami. Kami and Shinto always carried the label of animism, which someone looking for truth would immediately dismiss. That very day it became clear that there is a creator in Shinto who is acceptable to me.

Shinto Cosmology — Daigenrai and the One Spirit

The whole universe, the All, is represented in Shinto by a circle called Daikanu — "universe of the Great Circle." Like animism, Shinto looks for the spirit within each living thing, but also sees the origin and source of these 'many' in One Spirit (ichi-rei). Since ichirei is the Oneness of the origin and source of the All, it is called Daigenrai — the Great Original Spirit, "original kami", "universal organic body" or "creator kami of the universe."

Yamakage teaches that the "Big Circle of the Universe" is a current of whirling spirits springing out of Daigenrai. Individuals with identity are sparks of energy, also "manifested as a vortex." We are a child-spirit (bunrei) of this "core Spirit."

Kami is the spirit which pervades the entire universe, and man is the focus of the workings of heaven and earth. When Kami and man become one, infinite power will become manifest. Observing the true form of heaven and earth, we see the substance of the true Kami. — Onisaburo Deguchi, Omoto co-founder

The Kojiki — First God

Now when chaos had begun to condense, but force and form were not yet manifest, and there was nought named, nought done — Heaven and Earth first parted, and the Three Deities [Ame-no-mi-naka-nushi-no-kami, Taka-mi-musu-bi-no-kami, Kami-musu-bi-no-kami] performed the commencement of creation; the Passive and Active Essences then developed, and the Two Spirits became the ancestors of all things.

— The Kojiki (Records of Ancient Matters, 712 AD)

Ame-no-mi-naka-nushi-no-kami = Master of the August Centre of Heaven. He is the central kami for everything — the entire circle of the whole circle of the universe (Daikanu). He is the fountainhead kami, first in the divine lineage.
